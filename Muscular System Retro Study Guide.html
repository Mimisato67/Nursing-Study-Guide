Nerve Impulse & Contraction
p.203
5Where do muscle cells have a greater concentration of sodium ions?
Outside the cell membrane, compared to inside the cell.
p.203
6What is acetylcholine, and where will you find it?
A neurotransmitter substance found in the axon. Acetylcholine binds to receptors on the muscle, opening sodium ion channels.
As the nerve impulse of an axon reaches the neuromuscular junction, it triggers the release of acetylcholine.
p.203
7Describe the neuroelectrical pathway in skeletal muscle that leads to the release of calcium during an action potential.
A nerve impulse travels down a motor neuron to the neuromuscular junction, triggering release of acetylcholine into the synaptic cleft. Acetylcholine binds to receptors on the muscle cell, generating an action potential that travels along the muscle fiber into the T-tubules. This stimulates the sarcoplasmic reticulum to release calcium ions (Ca²⁺) into the sarcoplasm.
p.204
8What is the relationship between myosin, actin, and calcium?
When calcium is released, it binds to troponin on the actin filament, causing tropomyosin to shift and expose active sites on actin. Myosin heads attach to those sites, forming cross bridges and pulling the actin filaments inward — this is the sliding filament mechanism that produces muscle contraction.
Muscle Energy & Fatigue
p.64-65
9Describe the relationship between pyruvic acid and lactic acid.
During intense exercise, when oxygen is insufficient, pyruvic acid (the end product of glycolysis) is converted to lactic acid. This allows ATP production to continue temporarily without oxygen (anaerobic respiration). Lactic acid buildup causes the burning sensation in muscles and contributes to muscle fatigue.
p.19
10What is phosphocreatine, and where will you find it?
A high-energy compound stored in skeletal muscle cells. It provides a rapid reserve of energy by donating its phosphate group to ADP to quickly regenerate ATP during the first few seconds of intense muscle activity.
Muscle Tissue Types
p.198
11Where is smooth muscle found?
In the walls of hollow organs — e.g. stomach, intestines, urinary bladder, uterus, and blood vessels.
p.198
12Explain cardiac muscle.
Found only in the heart. It is involuntary and striated, and has intercalated discs that allow electrical impulses to spread rapidly between cells, enabling the heart to contract as a coordinated unit. It is self-stimulating (autorhythmic) and does not fatigue.
Cardiac muscle is controlled by the autonomic nervous system (ANS).
Muscle Naming & Actions

13What muscles are named according to their action?
Examples: flexor (flexes a joint), extensor (extends a joint), adductor (moves toward the body), abductor (moves away from the body), levator (raises a body part).

Brachialis, biceps brachii, and brachioradialis.

18What group of muscles extend the wrist in conjunction with what other muscle?
Extensor carpi radialis brevis, extensor carpi radialis longus, extensor carpi ulnaris, and extensor digitorum communis.

The rectus abdominis — the paired vertical muscle running along the front of the abdomen.

20What is special about the diaphragm?
It is the primary muscle of breathing. Its contracting causes air to enter the lungs.
