Why is preventing CAUTI important?
▼

CAUTI (catheter-associated urinary tract infection) occurs while an indwelling catheter is in place or up to 48 hours after discontinuing it.

Why It Matters
  • One of the most common healthcare-associated infections (HAIs)
  • Can progress to sepsis, prolonged hospitalization, and death
  • Older adults may only show atypical signs: confusion, falls, fever, tachycardia, hypotension
  • Most CAUTIs are preventable through aseptic technique and minimizing catheter use/dwell time
Risk Factors (Ch. 44)
  • Use of indwelling urinary catheters
  • Increased dwell time
  • Opening the closed drainage system
  • Routine irrigation of the catheter

6
Four interventions to prevent or manage constipation.
▼
  1. Increase fiber and water consumption (unless contraindicated) before more invasive interventions
  2. Give bulk-forming products before stool softeners, stimulants, or suppositories
  3. Encourage regular exercise (walking 15–20 min/day; exercises in bed for immobile clients)
  4. Enemas are a last resort for stimulating defecation
Causes of Constipation (Ch. 43)
  • Frequent use of laxatives · Advanced age · Inadequate fluid/fiber intake
  • Immobilization · Sedentary lifestyle · Pregnancy · Medications

Diarrhea — Most common complication, signs of dehydration, and beverages to avoid.
▼
🚨 Most Common Complication of Prolonged DiarrheaDehydration — leads to fluid and electrolyte disturbances (metabolic acidosis from excessive loss of bicarbonate)
Three Signs of Dehydration
  • Weak, rapid pulse / hypotension
  • Poor skin turgor
  • Elevated body temperature / dry mucous membranes
Electrolyte Imbalances from Diarrhea
  • Hypokalemia: leg cramps, muscle weakness, nausea, cardiac dysrhythmias
  • Hypernatremia: muscle weakness, lethargy, swollen red tongue
Beverages That Contribute to Diarrhea
  • Caffeinated beverages (coffee, tea, colas, energy drinks)
  • Alcohol

🦠 Infection Control — Ch. 11
9
Four examples of healthcare-associated infections (HAIs).
▼
  • Urinary tract infections (most common HAI — often from indwelling catheters)
  • Surgical wound infections
  • Respiratory tract infections (pneumonia)
  • Bloodstream infections (from IV lines/devices)
💡 Key FactThe best way to prevent HAIs is through frequent and effective hand hygiene. HAIs are not always preventable and not always iatrogenic. (Ch. 11)
10
Hand hygiene — Why it's most important + two principles of proper technique.
▼

Hands are the primary vehicle for transmitting pathogens. Hand hygiene breaks the chain of infection at the mode of transmission.

Two Principles (Ch. 11)
  • Alcohol-based waterless antiseptic is preferred when hands are NOT visibly soiled (more effective at removing micro-organisms)
  • Soap and water is used when hands ARE visibly soiled, contaminated with blood/body fluids, or when C. difficile is suspected (use soap/water, NOT alcohol for C. diff)

PPE chart — Contact, Droplet, and Airborne precautions.
▼
PrecautionPPE RequiredKey Diseases
ContactGloves + GownMRSA, VRSA, herpes simplex, scabies, C. diff, wound infections

Stages of infection in order + which stage occurs after exposure + which has significant S&S.
▼
StageDescription
1. IncubationInterval between pathogen entering the body and the first finding — occurs immediately after exposure
2. ProdromalOnset of general (non-specific) findings; pathogen multiplies during this stage
3. Illness (Acute)Significant, specific signs and symptoms of the infection occur here
4. DeclineManifestations begin to subside as number of pathogens decreases
5. ConvalescenceAcute findings disappear; total recovery (days to months)
💡 Quick AnswersStage after exposure = Incubation · Stage with significant S&S = Illness stage
14
Inflammation — Order of inflammatory response + four signs of localized inflammation.
▼
Inflammatory Response — Correct Order
  1. Recognition of harmful stimuli
  2. Activation of inflammatory cells
  3. Release of inflammatory markers
  4. Inflammatory pathways triggered
Four Signs of Localized Inflammation
  • Redness (erythema) — from dilation of arterioles bringing blood to the area
  • Warmth of the area on palpation
  • Edema (swelling)
  • Pain or tenderness of the affected part
Types of Exudate
  • Serous — clear fluid
  • Sanguineous — contains red blood cells
  • Purulent — contains leukocytes and bacteria
15
Safety & Sharps — Three methods for reducing needlestick injuries.
▼
  • Enable safety devices on all equipment and supplies immediately after use
  • Dispose of all sharps in a puncture-resistant container immediately after use
  • Never recap needles or leave sharps unattended
⚠️ Standard PrecautionsBag and handle contaminated laundry to prevent leaking. Remove gloves and perform hand hygiene between each client.

Vitamins & Nutrients — Deficiency findings. Basic nutrients the body requires.
▼
Nutrient DeficiencyPossible Finding
Vitamin CPoor wound healing, inflammation and bleeding of gums, dry scaly skin
FiberConstipation, change in bowel pattern, increased colorectal disease risk
ProteinFlaccid muscles, poor wound healing, loss of subcutaneous fat, weakness
Omega-3 Fatty AcidsIncreased inflammation, dry/brittle hair and nails, dry scaly skin
Basic Nutrients (Ch. 39)
  • Carbohydrates — main energy source; 4 kcal/gram; broken down to glucose
  • Fats — energy + vitamins; 9 kcal/gram; no more than 35% of caloric intake
  • Proteins — growth, maintenance, repair; 4 kcal/gram
  • Vitamins — fat-soluble (A, D, E, K) and water-soluble (C and B complex)
  • Minerals — calcium, potassium, sodium, iron

Hydration — Expected findings in dehydration. Dark urine causes.
▼
Expected Findings in Dehydration
  • Poor skin turgor
  • Dry mucous membranes
  • Weak, rapid pulse / hypotension
  • Dark-colored, concentrated urine
Dark-Colored Urine May Indicate
  • Dehydration / inadequate fluid intake
  • Medications: Phenazopyridine → orange/red; Levodopa → dark; Riboflavin → bright yellow; Amitriptyline → green-blue
  • Liver or biliary disease
  • Hematuria (blood in urine)
💡 Fluid Requirement (Ch. 39)Weigh clients daily at the same time, after voiding, wearing the same clothes. 1 oz = 30 mL when measuring I&O.
