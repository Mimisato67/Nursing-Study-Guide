🩺 Nursing Process — Chapter 7

Single Select
The nurse is caring for a client admitted with chest pain. Which step of the nursing process does the nurse perform FIRST?
💡 Rationale
B is correct. The nursing process always begins with Assessment/Data Collection — systematically collecting subjective and objective data to identify the client's needs. Planning, implementation, and evaluation follow assessment. (Ch. 7)
2
Single Select
A client tells the nurse, "My pain is a 9 out of 10 and nothing helps." The nurse documents this as which type of data?
💡 Rationale
A is correct. Subjective data (manifestations) includes what the client tells the nurse — feelings, perceptions, and descriptions of health status. Only the client can describe and verify their own manifestations. (Ch. 7)
3
Single Select
During the planning step, the nurse sets a client goal: "Client will ambulate 50 feet with a walker by day 3 without complaints of dizziness." Which characteristic of a well-written goal does this demonstrate?
💡 Rationale
D is correct. Ch. 7 states goals/outcomes must be client-centered, singular, observable, measurable, time-limited, mutually agreeable, and reasonable. The example meets all of these criteria.
4
Single Select
The nurse repositions a client every 2 hours to prevent skin breakdown without a specific provider order. This is an example of which type of nursing intervention?
💡 Rationale
A is correct. Nurse-initiated/independent interventions are autonomous actions nurses take using evidence-based rationale without a provider's order. Ch. 7 uses repositioning every 2 hours to prevent skin breakdown as the exact example.
5
Single Select
During the evaluation step, the nurse determines that the client did not meet the planned outcome. Which factor should the nurse consider FIRST as a reason for goal failure?
💡 Rationale
A is correct. Ch. 7 lists factors that can lead to lack of goal achievement: incomplete database, unrealistic client outcomes, nonspecific nursing interventions, and inadequate time. The nurse first evaluates the nursing process itself before blaming the client.

Single Select
Three clients need attention: Client A has a pain rating of 9/10, Client B needs a scheduled bath, and Client C reports dizziness upon standing. Using ABCDE prioritization, which client does the nurse assess FIRST?
💡 Rationale
C is correct. Dizziness upon standing indicates possible orthostatic hypotension or cardiovascular/neurological compromise — a circulation (C in ABCDE) emergency. This is a safety risk that requires immediate assessment. Bathing is a comfort need; pain is second priority.

🧠 Delegation & Clinical Judgment
8
Single Select
Which task is APPROPRIATE for the nurse to delegate to an unlicensed assistive personnel (UAP)?
💡 Rationale
C is correct. Vital signs on a stable client is a routine, predictable task appropriate for UAP. Assessment, medication administration, and care planning require nursing licensure and clinical judgment — these cannot be delegated.
9
Single Select
A nurse delegates bathing to the UAP. The nurse checks back 30 minutes later to see how the client tolerated the activity. This represents which Right of Delegation?
💡 Rationale
D is correct. Right Supervision means the nurse follows up after delegating to evaluate the outcome and ensure the task was completed safely. The nurse retains accountability for all delegated tasks.
10
Single Select
A nurse is using the Clinical Judgment Model. The nurse observes that a client's blood pressure has dropped from 130/80 to 90/60 mmHg and the client is diaphoretic. Which phase of the model is the nurse performing?
💡 Rationale
A is correct. Recognizing cues is noticing relevant client data — in this case the drop in BP and diaphoresis. Analyzing cues comes next (determining what it means), followed by prioritizing hypotheses and generating solutions.

Performing the initial client assessment
Assisting the client with ambulation
Administering oral medications
Developing the nursing care plan

🌿 Health Promotion & Disease Prevention — Chapter 16
12
Single Select
A nurse teaches a group of teenagers about the dangers of tobacco use and how to avoid peer pressure to smoke. This is an example of which level of prevention?
💡 Rationale
A is correct. Primary prevention addresses the needs of healthy clients to promote health and prevent disease with specific protections — decreasing exposure risk before disease occurs. Health education in schools is a primary prevention example from Ch. 16.
13
Single Select
A client with a recent spinal cord injury attends rehabilitation to learn how to manage daily activities and prevent complications. This is an example of which level of prevention?
💡 Rationale
C is correct. Tertiary prevention aims to prevent the long-term consequences of a chronic illness or disability and support optimal functioning. Ch. 16 lists rehabilitation after injury as a tertiary prevention example.
14
Single Select
A nurse is screening clients for diabetes at a community health fair. Clients who test positive are referred for treatment. This is an example of:
💡 Rationale
B is correct. Secondary prevention focuses on identifying illness, providing treatment, and conducting activities that help prevent worsening health status. Early detection and treatment of diabetes mellitus is a Ch. 16 secondary prevention example.
15
Single Select
A 45-year-old female client with no family history of colon cancer asks when she should have her first colonoscopy. The nurse's best response is:
💡 Rationale
A is correct. Ch. 16 states colorectal screening includes colonoscopy every 10 years for individuals not at increased risk, or high-sensitivity fecal occult blood testing every year between ages 45 and 75. All adults in this range should be screened regardless of family history.

SATA
A nurse is teaching a client about modifiable risk factors for cardiovascular disease. Which of the following are modifiable risk factors? (Select all that apply)
Tobacco use
Family history of heart disease
Sedentary lifestyle
High-fat diet
Age
Chronic stress
💡 Rationale
A, C, D, F are correct. Ch. 16 identifies lifestyle-risk behaviors clients have control over: tobacco use, diet, lack of exercise, and stress. Family history (genetics) and age are nonmodifiable — they cannot be changed but nurses help clients manage them.

Single Select
The Healthy People 2030 initiative is best described as:
💡 Rationale
B is correct. Ch. 16 describes Healthy People 2030 as a list of national objectives updated every 10 years to promote health and prevent disease among the national population through collaborative effort by governments, organizations, businesses, and individuals.

Single Select
A nurse is caring for a 21-year-old male client at a routine health visit. Which screening should the nurse anticipate the provider will recommend?
💡 Rationale
A is correct. Ch. 16 states testicular cancer is most common in males 15–34 years. Monthly testicular self-exam should be encouraged after puberty. Blood pressure is assessed at every health care visit. PSA starts at 55, blood glucose at 45, and colonoscopy at 45.
🦴 Mobility & Immobility — Chapter 40
19
Single Select
A client has been on bed rest for 5 days following surgery. Which complication related to immobility should the nurse monitor for FIRST?
💡 Rationale
B is correct. DVT is a life-threatening cardiovascular complication of immobility. Ch. 40 identifies stasis of blood in the legs and increased risk of thrombus development as key cardiovascular effects. DVT can lead to pulmonary embolism — a priority safety concern.
20
Single Select
A client is being taught to use a cane after a right hip replacement. The nurse instructs the client to hold the cane on which side?
💡 Rationale
B is correct. Ch. 40 Cane Instructions state: "Keep the cane on the stronger side of the body." This distributes weight away from the weaker/surgical leg and provides lateral support during ambulation.
21
Single Select
A client with left-sided weakness is learning to climb stairs with a cane. In which order should the nurse instruct the client to move when going UP the stairs?
💡 Rationale
C is correct. When going UP stairs: Strong leg goes first, then the cane and weak leg follow. Memory trick: "Up with the good, down with the bad." Going down, the weak leg and cane go first.
22
Single Select
Which principle of proper body mechanics should the nurse apply when transferring a client from the bed to a wheelchair?
💡 Rationale
D is correct. Proper body mechanics requires bending at the knees (not waist), using large muscle groups (legs), maintaining a wide base of support, and keeping the load close to the body. This protects the nurse's back and provides safe transfer for the client. (Ch. 40)
23
SATA
A client has been immobile for 1 week. Which complications should the nurse assess for? (Select all that apply)
Pressure injuries on bony prominences
Hypostatic pneumonia
Improved cardiac output
Urinary tract infection
Constipation
Muscle atrophy
💡 Rationale
A, B, D, E, F are correct. Ch. 40 lists systemic effects of immobility including pressure injuries (integumentary), hypostatic pneumonia (respiratory), UTI (genitourinary), constipation (GI), and muscle atrophy (musculoskeletal). Immobility decreases cardiac output — it does NOT improve it.
24
Single Select
The nurse is preparing to ambulate a client for the first time after 3 days of bed rest. Which action should the nurse perform FIRST?
💡 Rationale
C is correct. Before ambulating a client after bed rest, the nurse measures orthostatic blood pressure (lying → sitting → standing) to assess for orthostatic hypotension — a major cardiovascular complication of immobility (Ch. 40). This ensures client safety before standing.
25
Single Select
To prevent hypostatic pneumonia in an immobile client, which nursing intervention is MOST important?
💡 Rationale
B is correct. Ch. 40 respiratory nursing actions include repositioning every 1–2 hours, teaching the client to turn/cough/breathe deeply every 1–2 hours while awake, and using an incentive spirometer. Fluid intake should be at least 2,000 mL/day. Supine position worsens respiratory function.
26
Single Select
A client using a walker is instructed to advance the walker how far forward before stepping?
💡 Rationale
B is correct. Ch. 40 Walker Instructions state: advance walker forward 15–20 cm (6–8 in), then set all four feet down on the floor before stepping. This maintains stability and a safe center of gravity.
27
SATA
The nurse is planning care to promote mobility and prevent complications for a client on bed rest. Which interventions are appropriate? (Select all that apply)
Reposition the client every 1–2 hours
Encourage active or perform passive ROM 2–3 times daily
Apply sequential compression devices (SCDs)
Keep the client in the same position all day for comfort
Maintain fluid intake of at least 2,000 mL/day
Refer to physical therapy for decreased mobility
💡 Rationale
A, B, C, E, F are correct. All are Ch. 40 nursing actions for immobile clients. Keeping a client in the same position all day increases the risk of pressure injuries, DVT, contractures, and pneumonia — this is the incorrect option.
⭐ Priority & Mixed — All Topics

Client Scenario
🏥 A nurse is caring for four clients. Client 1: reports chronic low back pain rated 4/10. Client 2: just returned from surgery and has a BP of 88/50 mmHg. Client 3: needs discharge teaching about diet. Client 4: is requesting pain medication for a headache rated 3/10.
Using Maslow's hierarchy and ABCDE prioritization, which client should the nurse assess FIRST?
💡 Rationale
B is correct. A BP of 88/50 mmHg post-surgery indicates possible hemorrhage or shock — a life-threatening Circulation emergency (ABCDE). This is a physiological priority per Maslow's. Discharge teaching and mild/chronic pain are lower priorities. (Ch. 7)
29
Single Select
A nurse documents: "Client grimaced when turning in bed and rated pain 8/10." This is an example of which type of data?
💡 Rationale
C is correct. The pain rating of 8/10 is subjective data (what the client reports). The grimacing is objective data (what the nurse observes). Ch. 7: nurses observe/measure objective data during physical examination, while subjective data is what the client tells the nurse.
30
Single Select
A nurse notices an immobile client has redness over the sacrum that does not blanch. What is the nurse's priority intervention?
💡 Rationale
A is correct. Non-blanchable redness over a bony prominence is a Stage 1 pressure injury. Ch. 40 nursing actions include repositioning every 1–2 hours, using corrective positioning devices, and implementing a turning schedule. Massaging reddened areas is contraindicated — it damages fragile tissue.
31
Single Select
An older adult client is being discharged after a hip fracture repair. Which statement by the client indicates understanding of fall prevention?
💡 Rationale
D is correct. Ch. 40 fall prevention includes using assistive devices and non-slip footwear. Socks without grips increase fall risk. Getting up quickly risks orthostatic hypotension. Throw rugs are a trip hazard. The client should dangle at the bedside before standing.
32
Single Select
A client on a crutch with a three-point gait pattern has a fractured right leg. Which description of this gait pattern is correct?
💡 Rationale
B is correct. Ch. 40 defines three-point gait: requires bearing all weight on one foot while using both crutches. The affected leg should never bear weight or touch the ground. This is used when one leg is non-weight bearing (fractures, post-op).

SATA
The nurse is assessing a client's mobility. Which findings should the nurse include in the mobility assessment? (Select all that apply)
Range of motion of all joints
Gait and balance
Body alignment while standing, sitting, and lying
Client's insurance coverage
Activity tolerance and exercise status
Use of assistive devices
💡 Rationale
A, B, C, E, F are correct. Ch. 40 states assessment focuses on mobility, range of motion (ROM), gait, exercise status, activity tolerance, and body alignment while standing, sitting, and lying. Insurance coverage is not part of the mobility assessment.
34
Single Select
A nurse is educating a community group about how lifestyle factors affect health. Which statement by a participant indicates a NEED for further teaching?
💡 Rationale
C is correct (needs further teaching). Ch. 16 distinguishes modifiable risk factors (diet, exercise, smoking) from nonmodifiable ones (family history/genetics). While family history cannot be changed, clients CAN reduce their risk by controlling modifiable lifestyle factors — this statement shows a misconception.
35
Single Select
A nurse is caring for an immobile client who develops a negative nitrogen balance. Which systemic effect of immobility does this represent?
💡 Rationale
B is correct. Ch. 40 lists metabolic effects of immobility: negative nitrogen balance, decreased protein resulting in loss of muscle, decreased basal metabolic rate, altered endocrine system, and changes in protein, carbohydrate, and fat metabolism. High-calorie, high-protein diet with vitamins B and C is the nursing intervention.
