💊 Drug Names & Classification

Drug Names — Generic vs Trade

Generic Name

  • Official nonproprietary name
  • Written in lowercase
  • Assigned before approval
  • Bioequivalent to brand name but cheaper
  • Ex: ibuprofen

Trade/Brand Name

  • Given by manufacturer
  • Written Capitalized
  • May have multiple trade names
  • Ex: Advil, Motrin

Drug Class/Category

Drugs grouped together based on similar chemical structure, mechanism of action, or therapeutic use.

Examples: beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, opioids, statins

Controlled Substances & DEA Schedules

Drugs regulated by the federal government under the Controlled Substances Act (CSA) because they have potential for abuse, dependence, or addiction.

DEA = Drug Enforcement Administration — federal agency that enforces controlled substance laws and regulations in the U.S.

ScheduleAbuse PotentialExamples

Physical vs Psychological Dependence

Physical Dependence

Physiological state of adaptation where abrupt discontinuation causes withdrawal symptoms

Ex: tremors, nausea, seizures

Psychological Dependence

Emotional/mental craving for a drug's effects — compulsive need to use a substance even without physical need

⚗️ Pharmacology Phases

Drug Activity Within the Body

Process by which a drug tablet disintegrates and dissolves so it can be absorbed.

PhaseDefinitionKey Factors
Pharmaceutic1st phase — drug dissolved & disintegrated so it can be absorbedDrug form, enteric coating, pH, food
PharmacokineticHow the body processes the drug (ADME)See ADME below

ADME — Pharmacokinetic Phase

Absorption

Movement of drug from administration site into the bloodstream

Key factors: route, blood flow, drug solubility, GI motility

Distribution

Drug moves from blood into body tissues and fluids

Key factors: protein binding, blood-brain barrier, liquid solubility, body fat

Metabolism

Drug chemically changed (primarily in the liver) into active or inactive metabolites

Key factors: liver function, first-pass effect, enzyme activity

Excretion

Elimination of drug from the body (primarily kidneys/urine)

Key factors: renal function, pH of urine

Drug Action Terms

Onset

Time from administration until drug reaches minimum effective concentration and produces a therapeutic effect

Peak

Time when the drug reaches its highest concentration in the blood

Duration of Action

Length of time the drug maintains sufficient concentration to produce therapeutic effect

🔬 Drug Receptor Actions

Agonist vs Antagonist

Agonist

Drug that binds to a receptor and ACTIVATES it, producing a response

Ex: morphine on opioid receptors

Antagonist

Drug that binds to a receptor but BLOCKS the response without activating it

Ex: naloxone blocks opioid receptors

Teratogen

A substance that causes birth defects by interfering with fetal development

Examples: thalidomide, isotretinoin, alcohol

⚠️ FDA Pregnancy Categories A, B, C, D, X guide prescribing decisions

Pregnancy Considerations

  • All drugs are potentially harmful to the fetus, especially during 1st trimester (organogenesis)
  • Many drugs cross the placenta and reach fetal circulation
  • Avoid all non-essential medications during pregnancy
  • Always consult provider before taking any medication including OTC & herbal
⚠️ Drug Reactions & Safety

Adverse Drug Reactions (ADR)

Any unintended, harmful response to a drug given at the NORMAL therapeutic dose

Examples: nausea, rash, hepatotoxicity

Allergic Drug Reactions

Immune-mediated (antigen-antibody) response — ranges from mild rash/hives to life-threatening anaphylaxis

Antigen

Foreign substance (drug or pathogen) that the immune system identifies as foreign and triggers an immune response

Antibody

Protein produced by B cells in response to an antigen; designed to recognize and neutralize specific antigens

Signs & Symptoms of Allergic Reactions by System

SystemSigns & Symptoms
RespiratoryWheezing, bronchospasm, dyspnea, laryngeal edema, stridor
CardiovascularHypotension, tachycardia, cardiovascular collapse, weak/thready pulse
SkinUrticaria (hives), itching, rash, angioedema, flushing, pallor
GINausea, vomiting, abdominal cramping, diarrhea

Anaphylactic Shock — Treatment

🚨 Can be fatal — massive histamine release causes vasodilation, bronchospasm, circulatory collapse → respiratory failure and death if not treated immediately
  • Epinephrine (1st line) — administer IM immediately
  • Maintain airway, apply supplemental oxygen
  • IV fluid bolus to restore blood pressure
  • Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) IV/IM — antihistamine
  • Corticosteroids (methylprednisolone) — prevent recurrence
  • Activate emergency response / call 911

Drug Idiosyncrasy

Abnormal, unexpected reaction unique to an individual — not related to the drug's known pharmacology

Ex: excitation instead of sedation from a barbiturate

Drug Tolerance

Decreased response to a drug after repeated use, requiring higher doses to achieve the same effect

Examples: opioids, benzodiazepines, alcohol

Cumulative Drug Effect

Drug accumulates in the body faster than it can be metabolized or excreted → leads to toxicity

Common with patients who have renal or hepatic impairment

Toxic Reactions

Harmful effects from excessively high drug concentrations in the body. Can result from overdose, impaired metabolism, or drug accumulation.

LVN Role — Keeping Drug Within Therapeutic Range

  • Monitor drug blood levels as ordered (trough and peak)
  • Observe for signs of toxicity or sub-therapeutic effect
  • Assess renal/hepatic function regularly
  • Administer drugs at consistently scheduled times
  • Report abnormal lab values to RN/provider immediately
💬 Drug Interactions

Types of Drug Interactions

TypeEffectFormulaExample
AdditiveEffects add together1+1=2Two CNS depressants together
SynergisticEffect greater than sum1+1=3Alcohol + benzodiazepines
AntagonisticOne blocks the other1+1=0Naloxone blocking morphine

Drug-to-Drug: When 2 drugs interact and alter the effect of one or both. Ex: Warfarin + Aspirin = increased bleeding risk

Drug-to-Food: Food affects how a drug works — can make it more/less effective or cause dangerous reactions

Factors Influencing Drug Response

Disease

Liver/kidney disease impairs metabolism and excretion → increased drug levels and toxicity risk

Route

Affects absorption speed and bioavailability. IV = fastest, Oral = slowest

Weight

Body weight and composition affect drug distribution; higher weight may require higher doses

Age

Elderly have reduced kidney/liver function; pediatric patients metabolize drugs differently

Genetics

Genetic variations affect enzyme activity (pharmacogenomics); some patients are rapid vs. slow metabolizers

Polypharmacy

Concurrent use of 5+ medications by one patient; increases risk of drug interactions; most common in elderly

Drug reactions are: Unpredictable & Individualized

📋 Drug History & Administration

Drug History — What to Ask the Patient

  • All current prescriptions (name, dose, frequency)
  • OTC meds (pain relievers, antacids, cold meds)
  • Herbal/dietary supplement use
  • Past adverse/unusual drug reactions (allergies)
  • Alcohol, tobacco, and recreational drug use
  • Compliance with current meds (any missed doses?)
  • Drug, food, and environmental allergies (type of reaction)

7 Rights of Drug Administration

#RightAction
1Right PatientVerify 2 identifiers (name + DOB or MRN)
2Right DrugCompare drug label to MAR three times
3Right DoseCalculate carefully; verify with pharmacist for high-alert drugs
4Right RouteConfirm correct administration route
5Right TimeAdminister at scheduled time; follow frequency rules
6Right DocumentationDocument AFTER giving the drug — NEVER before
7Right ReasonConfirm drug is appropriate for the diagnosis

Considerations Before Drug Administration

  • Check allergies
  • Verify 6+1 rights
  • Assess client's ability to take the medication (swallow, IV patency, etc.)
  • Check contraindications and precautions
  • Know the drug before giving it

What nurse must know before giving:

  • 1. Indications — why is this drug ordered?
  • 2. Contraindications/Precautions — is it safe?
  • 3. Adverse effects — what do I monitor for?
  • 4. Correct dose, route & time — am I giving it correctly?
  • 5. Drug interactions — any conflicts with food, meds or supplements?

Preparing a Drug — Key Steps

  • Check & verify MAR (Medication Administration Record)
  • Check label 3 times: when pulling, when preparing, when administering
  • Verify patient allergies before each administration
  • Calculate dose correctly; have another nurse double-check high-alert drugs
  • Check expiration date on all medications
  • Use proper aseptic technique for parenteral medications
  • Never crush enteric-coated or sustained-release tablets
  • Do not transfer drug to different containers

Preventing Medication Errors

Any preventable event that may cause or lead to inappropriate medication use or patient harm

  • Follow facility policies/procedures
  • Question unclear orders
  • Be alert to SALAD drugs (Sound-Alike/Look-Alike Drugs)
  • Document immediately after administration
  • Never leave medication unattended
  • Insulin & Heparin — 2 drugs associated with deadly/adverse outcomes

Bar Coded Medication Systems & Unit Dose

Unit Dose

Each medication dose is individually packaged and labeled for single administration — reduces errors, minimizes waste, improves accountability

Bar Coded System — 3 Scans

  • 1. Patient wristband (identity)
  • 2. Medication barcode (drug, dose, route)
  • 3. Nurse's ID badge (authorized nurse)

After Giving a Drug — Nurse Responsibilities

  • 1. Document — time, dose, route, site, and client response

  • 4. Teach patient what to expect
🩺 Glucocorticoids (Adrenocortical Hormones)

Glucocorticoids Overview

Class of adrenocortical hormones (steroids) either naturally produced by the adrenal cortex or synthetically made.

Actions

  • Anti-inflammatory — suppress inflammatory response
  • Immunosuppressive — decrease immune system activity
  • Suppress adrenal cortex function (long-term)
  • Increase blood glucose (gluconeogenesis)
  • Cause sodium and water retention

Uses

  • Inflammatory conditions (arthritis, IBD, asthma)
  • Allergic reactions/anaphylaxis
  • Adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease)
  • Organ transplant rejection prevention
  • Autoimmune disorders (lupus, MS)
  • Cerebral edema

Glucocorticoids — Adverse Reactions

  • Cushing's syndrome (moon face, buffalo bump, central obesity)
  • Hyperglycemia
  • Osteoporosis
  • Immunosuppression → increased infection risk
  • Peptic ulcers / GI bleeding
  • Adrenal insufficiency if stopped abruptly
  • Poor wound healing
  • Cataracts/glaucoma (long-term)
  • Hypertension
  • Mood changes, insomnia, psychosis

Glucocorticoids — Drug Interactions

DrugEffect
NSAIDsIncreased GI bleeding risk
Diuretics (thiazide/loop)Increased hypokalemia risk
Antidiabetic agentsDecreased effectiveness (steroids raise blood glucose)
DigoxinIncreased toxicity risk (hypokalemia amplifies digoxin effect)
AspirinIncreased GI ulceration

Glucocorticoids — Nursing Alert & Client Education

🚨 NEVER stop abruptly — always TAPER to prevent adrenal crisis

Nursing Monitoring

  • Never stop abruptly — taper dose
  • Monitor blood glucose frequently
  • Monitor for signs of Cushing's
  • Monitor for infection (steroids mask fever)
  • Monitor weight, BP, electrolytes
  • Adrenal crisis signs: hypotension, weakness, N/V, confusion

Client Education

  • Never stop abruptly — taper as directed
  • Take with food, in the morning
  • Wear medical alert bracelet
  • Avoid live vaccines
  • Report signs of infection immediately
  • Monitor glucose if diabetic

Glucocorticoids — Gerontologic Considerations

  • Use lowest effective dose for shortest time
  • Decreased renal/hepatic function → drug accumulates → toxicity risk
  • Higher risk for osteoporosis, hypertension, hyperglycemia, infection
  • Increased fall risk from muscle weakness
