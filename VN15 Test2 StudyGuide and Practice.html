Vital Signs · Assessment Data · Cardiovascular · Oxygenation · Oral Care

Assessment Data
1. Explain subjective data and provide a few examples.
SubjectiveSubjective data is the client's verbal report about their own health history — what the client tells the nurse. It cannot be measured or observed directly by another person.
  • "I feel nauseous."
  • "My chest hurts."
  • "I have pain in my lower back."
2. Explain objective data and provide a few examples.
ObjectiveObjective data is collected through diagnostic tests and physical assessments — measurable, observable facts that can be verified by another person.
  • Patient BP 120/80 mmHg
  • Patient O₂ Sat is 99%
  • Temperature 37.2°C (99°F)
3. What are primary sources of assessment data?
The primary sources of assessment data are the client and the nurse's own observation. Secondary sources include family members, other healthcare providers, and medical records.
Documentation of Vital Signs
4. What information needs to be documented when conducting each of the following:
a. Pulse — Measurement of heart rate (HR) and rhythm. Corresponds to the bounding of blood flowing through various points in the circulatory system. Document: rate, rhythm, strength/quality.
b. Blood Pressure — Reflects the force the blood exerts against the walls of arteries during cardiac muscle contraction (systole) and relaxation (diastole). Document: systolic/diastolic values, which arm, position of patient, cuff size used.
c. Temperature — Reflects the body's internal and external temperature. Document: value, unit (°C or °F), and route used (oral, rectal, axillary, tympanic, temporal).
Factors Affecting Vital Signs — Higher & Lower Values
5. Identify what issues would cause a patient to have higher and lower values:
a. Pulse
Higher Fever, medication, acute pain, hyperthyroidism, anemia, hypoxemia, stress/anxiety/fear, hypovolemia, shock, heart failure, hemorrhage, changing positions.
Lower Long-term physical fitness, hypothermia, medication, chronic severe pain, relaxation.
b. Blood Pressure
Higher Older adults, stress, ethnicity, menopause, adolescence in males, medications, obesity, family history, high sodium intake.
Lower Exercise (long-term effect), circadian rhythm, infants have lower BP.
c. Temperature
Higher Dehydration, heat exhaustion, fever, vital organ damage, heat stroke.
Lower Nervous system depression, metabolism changes, environment, cardiovascular and respiratory distress.
Temperature Measurement Methods
6. Explain the various methods of taking a temperature.
Methods: Forehead (temporal artery), Oral, Rectal, Armpit (axillary), Ear (tympanic)
  • When to use one over another: Alternative methods are used when other body parts are not accessible or depending on age, health status, and current status.
  • Fever in an adult: 100.4°F (38°C) oral temperature or higher. Actions: Keep patient hydrated, encourage rest, make sure patient is warm and comfortable.
Cardiovascular Assessment
7. What data is collected when performing a cardiovascular assessment?
A mix of both objective and subjective data: patient history, physical measurements, and diagnostic results.
  • a. How to take BP: Back support, uncrossed feet, correct cuff size, cuff 2 inches above the elbow, arm rested at heart level.
  • b. Apical heart rate location: Left midclavicular line at the 5th intercostal space (ICS). Listen for a full 60 seconds.
  • c. Hypertension: Constant elevated BP readings, commonly above 120/80 mmHg.
  • d. Hypotension: BP reading lower than 90/60 mmHg. Seek help or treat if associated with other issues.
  • e. Irregular heartbeat: Palpitations (>100 bpm) or slow HR (<60 bpm). Caused by premature, late, or missed heartbeats creating irregular intervals.
  • f. Normal pulse range: 60–100 beats/min for an adult at rest.
  • g. Pulse deficit: Beat at apical site with no pulsation at the radial pulse point. Apical pulse > peripheral pulse.
  • h. Postural/Orthostatic hypotension: Same condition, different names. BP drops suddenly when changing positions.
Teeth & Oral Care
8. What information does a nurse need to know regarding teeth and oral care?
Teeth and oral care are important for digestion and peristalsis. Poor oral hygiene can increase risk of aspiration, pneumonia, and diabetes complications. It also allows the patient to feel clean and comfortable.
Oxygen Saturation
9. How does a nurse obtain oxygen saturation on a client?
Place an oximeter probe on the patient's vascular area — commonly the fingers, toes, or earlobe — for about 10–30 seconds.
  • a. Low O₂ Sat: Reading drops below 92%. Physical signs: shortness of breath, chest pain, lung/heart conditions.
  • b. Action for low O₂ Sat: Verify the reading, sit patient up, assess for cyanosis or confusion, provide supplemental oxygen per protocol.

— Part 2: Blood Pressure —
5
A nurse is preparing to assess a client's blood pressure. Which action is correct? BP
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: B. Proper BP technique includes back support, uncrossed feet, correct cuff size, cuff 2 inches above the elbow, and arm rested at heart level. Crossing legs can falsely elevate BP. Wrong cuff size gives inaccurate readings. Raising the arm above heart level falsely lowers the reading.
6
A client's blood pressure is 86/54 mmHg and they report feeling lightheaded when standing. The nurse recognizes this as: BP
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: C. BP below 90/60 mmHg indicates hypotension. When this occurs upon position changes with dizziness, it is orthostatic (postural) hypotension — both terms describe the same condition. Hypertension is consistently elevated BP. A pulse deficit involves a discrepancy between apical and peripheral pulse rates.
7
Which finding would the nurse recognize as indicating hypertension? BP
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: C. Hypertension is defined as constant elevated BP readings, commonly above 120/80 mmHg — it must be consistent across multiple readings. A BP of 88/58 (A) indicates hypotension. 118/76 (B) is within normal limits. Post-exercise BP changes (D) are transient and expected.
8
Which client condition would most likely cause a DECREASE in blood pressure? BP
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: C. Factors that LOWER BP include long-term exercise, circadian rhythm, and infants having lower BP. Obesity with high sodium (A), chronic stress (B), and menopause (D) all contribute to HIGHER BP. Regular aerobic exercise strengthens the heart and reduces vascular resistance.

SATA

A drop to 94/62 upon standing, accompanied by dizziness, is a classic presentation. It is not hypertension (BP is not elevated), tachycardia (pulse rate not addressed), or a pulse deficit.
— Part 3: Pulse & Heart Rate —
11
What is the expected normal pulse range for an adult at rest? Pulse
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: C. The normal adult resting pulse is 60–100 beats per minute. Below 60 bpm = bradycardia; above 100 bpm = tachycardia. Well-trained athletes may normally have a resting HR below 60 without it being pathological.
12
A nurse counts 92 beats per minute at the apical site but only palpates 76 beats per minute at the radial pulse. The nurse should document this as: Pulse
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: D. A pulse deficit occurs when there is a beat at the apical site with NO corresponding pulsation at the peripheral (radial) pulse — meaning apical pulse > peripheral pulse. Some cardiac contractions are not strong enough to produce a palpable wave at the wrist. This can signal atrial fibrillation or other cardiac conditions.
13
Which client is MOST likely to have an elevated pulse rate? Pulse
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: C. Factors that INCREASE pulse include fever, acute pain, hyperthyroidism, anemia, hypoxemia, stress/anxiety, hypovolemia, shock, heart failure, and hemorrhage. Long-term physical fitness (A), beta-blockers (B), and relaxation (D) all LOWER the pulse rate.
14
A client's HR is 118 beats/min with an irregular rhythm. The client reports "feeling their heart flutter." The nurse recognizes this as: Pulse
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: D. HR >100 bpm = tachycardia. Palpitations occur when a premature, late, or missed heartbeat causes an irregular interval between impulses. "Heart fluttering" is a common patient description of palpitations. An irregular heartbeat at this rate requires further assessment and provider notification.
15
Where does the nurse place the stethoscope to auscultate the apical heart rate? Cardiovascular
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: B. The apical heart rate is found at the left midclavicular line at the 5th intercostal space (ICS) — the point of maximal impulse (PMI). This is where the apex of the heart is closest to the chest wall. Listen for a full 60 seconds for accuracy.
16
Which factors would cause a client's pulse to be LOWER than expected? Select all that apply. Pulse
SATA
✏️ Rationale
Correct: A, C, E. Factors that LOWER pulse: long-term physical fitness (A), hypothermia (C), certain medications, chronic severe pain, and relaxation (E). Fever (B), acute pain (D), and hemorrhage (F) all INCREASE the pulse rate due to increased cardiac demand.
— Part 4: Temperature —
17
A client's oral temperature is 38.4°C. What is the nurse's PRIORITY action? Temperature
Priority
✏️ Rationale
Correct: B. 38.4°C exceeds the fever threshold of 38°C (100.4°F). Priority nursing actions for fever: keep the patient hydrated, encourage rest, and ensure the patient is warm and comfortable. Administering antipyretics (A) requires a provider order. Never discharge a febrile patient without full assessment (D).
18
A nurse is choosing a temperature method for a 3-month-old infant. Which is MOST appropriate? Temperature
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: D. Alternative temperature methods are used based on age, health status, and accessibility. Oral is inappropriate for infants. Tympanic (B) is less accurate in infants under 6 months due to small curved ear canals. Rectal is most accurate for infants; temporal artery is also common and less invasive.
19
A client has been in the sun for several hours without fluids. Their temperature is 40.1°C. The nurse should assess for which PRIORITY complication? Temperature
Priority
✏️ Rationale
Correct: C. Causes of HIGH body temperature include dehydration, heat exhaustion, fever, vital organ damage, and heat stroke. A client who is dehydrated with prolonged heat exposure and a temp of 40.1°C is at high risk for heat stroke — a medical emergency. Respiratory depression (D) is a cause of LOW temperature.

Which of the following are recognized methods for measuring body temperature? Select all that apply. Temperature
SATA

Correct: A, B, D, E, F. The five recognized temperature measurement methods are: Oral (A), Rectal (B), Tympanic/ear (D), Temporal artery/forehead (E), and Axillary/armpit (F). The brachial artery (C) is used for blood pressure measurement, not temperature.
21
Which factor would most likely cause a client's body temperature to be LOWER than expected? Temperature
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: B. Causes of LOW body temperature include nervous system depression, metabolism changes, environment, and cardiovascular and respiratory distress. Dehydration/sun exposure (A), active infection (C), and heat exhaustion (D) all cause ELEVATED temperatures.
— Part 5: Oxygen Saturation —
22
A client's O₂ saturation reads 89%. Which action should the nurse take FIRST? Oxygenation
Priority
✏️ Rationale
Correct: B. O₂ Sat below 92% requires immediate action. Priority: verify the reading (check probe placement), sit the patient upright to improve lung expansion, and restore oxygenation by applying supplemental oxygen per protocol. Waiting (A, C) risks hypoxia. Assessment alone (D) is insufficient when O₂ is critically low.
23
Where does a nurse place the pulse oximeter probe to obtain an accurate O₂ saturation reading? Oxygenation
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: C. The oximeter probe is placed on the patient's vascular area — commonly the fingers, toes, or earlobe — for about 10–30 seconds. These sites have adequate capillary blood flow for the light sensor to calculate oxygen saturation accurately.
24
A nurse is assessing a client for possible low oxygen saturation. Which findings would indicate low O₂ saturation? Select all that apply. Oxygenation
SATA
✏️ Rationale
Correct: A, B, D, E. Low O₂ saturation (below 92%) is indicated by: O₂ Sat <92% (A), shortness of breath (B), cyanosis — bluish discoloration (D), and chest pain (E). A BP of 130/84 (C) may indicate hypertension but not directly low O₂ Sat. A normal HR and rhythm (F) is not a sign of low O₂.
25
A client's O₂ Sat drops to 90% and they appear confused and cyanotic. After verifying the reading, the nurse's NEXT action is to: Oxygenation
Priority
✏️ Rationale
Correct: C. With confirmed low O₂ Sat, confusion, and cyanosis, the nurse must restore oxygenation immediately: sit the client upright (improves lung expansion and diaphragm movement) and apply supplemental oxygen per protocol. Laying flat (B) would worsen respiratory effort. Acting without orders (D) is outside scope without a protocol. Waiting (A) is unsafe.

Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: B. Teeth and oral care support digestion and peristalsis. Poor oral hygiene can increase the risk of serious systemic conditions such as aspiration pneumonia and diabetes. Oral care is important for ALL clients, including those on NPO status or tube feeding.

A nurse is caring for a client on a ventilator. Why is oral hygiene ESPECIALLY important for this client? Oral Care
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: C. Poor teeth/oral hygiene can increase risk of severe systemic conditions such as aspiration pneumonia and diabetes. Ventilated clients are at extremely high risk for ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP), which is directly linked to oral bacterial colonization. Oral care should be done every 2–4 hours per protocol in ventilated patients.

Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: C. Normal adult resting pulse = 60–100 bpm. A HR of 52 bpm is below 60 bpm, which is bradycardia. Note: well-trained athletes may have a resting HR below 60 without it being pathological, so clinical context matters. This requires assessment and documentation.

The nurse is documenting a client's vital signs. Which information is MOST important to include when recording a temperature? Temperature
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: D. When documenting temperature, you must record the value, unit (°C or °F), and the route/location used (oral, rectal, axillary, tympanic, temporal). The route matters because different sites yield slightly different readings — rectal is highest, axillary is lowest.
30
A client has been diagnosed with anemia. How would the nurse expect this to affect the client's pulse? Pulse
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: B. Anemia is listed as a cause of ELEVATED pulse rate. When there are fewer red blood cells to carry oxygen, the heart compensates by beating faster to deliver more blood to tissues. This is a compensatory tachycardia. The nurse should monitor closely for signs of inadequate oxygenation.

A nurse is preparing to document a client's pulse. Which information should be included? Select all that apply. Pulse
SATA
✏️ Rationale
Correct: A, B, D. When documenting a pulse assessment, the nurse should record: rate in beats per minute (A), rhythm — regular or irregular (B), and strength/quality of the pulse (D). Blood type (C) and last meal time (E) are not relevant to pulse documentation.
32
A client reports feeling anxious about an upcoming procedure and their pulse is 108 bpm. The nurse understands that the elevated pulse is MOST likely due to: Pulse
Single
✏️ Rationale
Correct: C. Stress, anxiety, and fear are listed as causes of elevated pulse rate. When anxious, the sympathetic nervous system triggers a "fight or flight" response, releasing catecholamines that increase heart rate. This is a normal physiological response. Hypothermia (A) and relaxation (B) lower the pulse. Beta-blockers lower pulse but the client is anxious, not medicated (D).

Obesity + family history + high sodium (D) is a triple-risk combination. Infants (A) typically have lower BP. Exercise (B) and low sodium (C) lower BP.
