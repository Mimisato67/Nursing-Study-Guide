Q2

Generic vs. Trade drug names — what's the difference?

▾
  • Generic name: Official, nonproprietary name — lowercase, assigned before FDA approval (e.g., ibuprofen)
  • Trade name: Brand name given by the manufacturer — capitalized, may have multiple trade names per generic (e.g., Advil, Motrin)
  • Key point: Generics are bioequivalent to brand-name drugs but significantly cheaper

Pharmacokinetics — ADME

Q4

Explain the four phases of pharmacokinetics (ADME)

▾
PhaseDefinitionKey Factors
A — AbsorptionDrug moves from administration site → bloodstreamRoute, blood flow, drug solubility, GI motility
D — DistributionDrug moves from blood → body tissues/fluidsProtein binding, blood-brain barrier, lipid solubility
M — MetabolismDrug chemically changed (primarily in the liver)Liver function, first-pass effect, enzyme activity
E — ExcretionDrug eliminated from body — mainly via kidneysRenal function, urine pH, biliary excretion
⚠️ Nursing Alert: Clients with kidney or liver disease require careful monitoring — impaired metabolism/excretion leads to drug accumulation and toxicity.
  • Onset: Time until drug reaches minimum effective concentration
  • Peak: Time of highest drug concentration in blood
  • Duration of action: Length of time therapeutic effect is maintained
  • First-pass metabolism: Large portion of oral dose metabolized by liver BEFORE reaching systemic circulation — reduces bioavailability
Q5

Define: Agonist, Antagonist, Teratogen

▾
  • Agonist: Binds to a receptor and ACTIVATES it → produces a response (e.g., morphine on opioid receptors)
  • Antagonist: Binds to a receptor but BLOCKS the response without activating it (e.g., naloxone blocks opioid receptors)
  • Teratogen: Substance causing birth defects by interfering with fetal development (e.g., thalidomide, isotretinoin, alcohol)
🚫 Pregnancy: ALL drugs potentially harmful to fetus — especially during first trimester (organogenesis). Many cross placenta. Always consult provider before any medication, including OTC/herbal.

Q7

Drug Tolerance, Cumulative Effect, Idiosyncrasy & Toxic Reactions

▾
  • Drug Tolerance: Decreased response after repeated use → higher doses needed for same effect (opioids, benzodiazepines)
  • Cumulative Drug Effect: Drug accumulates faster than it can be metabolized/excreted → toxicity. Common in renal/hepatic impairment
  • Idiosyncrasy: Abnormal UNEXPECTED reaction unique to individual (e.g., excitation from a barbiturate instead of sedation)
  • Toxic Reaction: Harmful effects from excessively high drug concentration — overdose, accumulation, or impaired elimination
LVN Role — Maintaining Therapeutic Range: Monitor drug blood levels, observe for toxicity signs, check renal/hepatic labs, give drugs on schedule, report abnormal values immediately.

Drug Interactions & Factors Influencing Response

Q8

Types of Drug Interactions

▾
TypeEffectExample
Additive1 + 1 = 2 (sum of effects)Two CNS depressants
Synergistic1 + 1 = 3 (greater than sum)Alcohol + benzodiazepines
Antagonistic1 + 1 = 0 (one blocks other)Naloxone blocking morphine
Drug to FoodFood alters absorption/metabolismGrapefruit juice + statins

D.R.W.A.G — Factors Influencing Drug Response:

  • D — Disease: Liver/kidney disease impairs metabolism and excretion
  • R — Route: Fastest = IV; Slowest = Oral
  • W — Weight: Higher weight may require higher doses
  • A — Age: Elderly = START LOW, GO SLOW; Pediatric = weight-based dosing
  • G — Genetics: Rapid vs. slow metabolizers (pharmacogenomics)
Herbal supplements: NOT regulated by the FDA. Always ask about herbal use in drug history — significant interactions exist (St. John's Wort reduces warfarin and OCP effectiveness; Garlic increases bleeding risk).

Q11

Medication Safety Systems — BCMA, Unit Dose & Do Not Use List

▾
  • HIGH-ALERT drugs most associated with fatal errors: Insulin & Heparin
  • Unit Dose: Each dose individually packaged and labeled — reduces errors and waste
  • BCMA (Bar Code Medication Administration): Scan patient wristband + medication barcode + nurse ID badge before every administration
🚫 Do Not Use Abbreviations (Joint Commission):
• "U" for units → can be misread as "0" (10-fold overdose)
• "IU" → can be mistaken for IV
• Trailing zero (1.0 mg) → write as 1 mg
• Naked decimal (.5 mg) → write as 0.5 mg
• "QD" and "QOD" → write out "daily" and "every other day"

Glucocorticoids (Adrenocortical Hormones)

Q12

Glucocorticoids — Actions, Uses, Adverse Reactions & Interactions

▾
CategoryDetails
ActionsAnti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive, increase blood glucose, promote Na/water retention, suppress HPA axis (long-term)
UsesAutoimmune disorders, asthma/COPD, severe allergic reactions, transplant rejection prevention, Addison's disease, cancer adjunct
Adverse ReactionsHyperglycemia, Cushing's syndrome (moon face, buffalo hump), osteoporosis, immunosuppression, hypertension, peptic ulcers, mood changes, adrenal suppression
ContraindicationsActive fungal infections, live vaccines, hypersensitivity. Caution: diabetes, peptic ulcers, hypertension, osteoporosis
Key Drug InteractionsNSAIDs → increased GI bleeding; Diuretics → worsened hypokalemia; Insulin/oral agents → hyperglycemia; Live vaccines → severe infection risk
🚨 NURSING ALERT — Never abruptly stop glucocorticoids!
Abrupt discontinuation causes ADRENAL CRISIS — a life-threatening emergency. Always taper dose gradually. Monitor glucose, BP, weight, and infection signs. Give WITH FOOD to reduce GI upset.

Gerontology, Diabetes & Nursing Process

Q13

Gerontologic Considerations in Pharmacology

▾
  • Decreased renal and hepatic function → slower metabolism and excretion → drug accumulation
  • Higher risk for toxicity — START LOW, GO SLOW with dosing
  • Polypharmacy common — increases drug interaction risk significantly
  • Reduced albumin → more free (unbound) drug circulating → stronger effects
  • May have impaired vision, dexterity, or cognition affecting self-administration
  • Increased body fat → prolonged effect of fat-soluble drugs
Q14

Health Literacy Assessment — Teach-Back Method

▾
  • Teach-Back Method: Ask patient to explain instructions back in their OWN words — gold standard for assessing comprehension
  • NVS (Newest Vital Sign): Patient reads a nutrition label and answers 6 questions — screens literacy in ~3 minutes
  • Signs of low health literacy: Difficulty completing forms, avoids written materials, inconsistent medication use, misses appointments
  • Strategies: Plain language, 5th–6th grade reading level, visual aids, professional translators, short focused sessions
Key: Recommended reading level for patient materials is 5th–6th grade. Always ask open-ended questions: "Tell me how you take your blood pressure medication" not "Do you understand?"
