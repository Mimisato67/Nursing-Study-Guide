🏗 Skin Layers
1
Describe the layers of the skin.
▼
LayerDescription
EpidermisSuperficial (outermost) layer of the skin; composed of epithelial tissue
DermisSecond layer; thick layer of dense connective tissue that connects the skin below it; contains hair follicles, glands, nerves, and blood vessels
HypodermisBelow the dermis; composed of adipose (fat) tissue; also called subcutaneous layer
💡 Memory TrickEDH — Epidermis · Dermis · Hypodermis (outside → inside)
2
Define all the stratums (layers) of the epidermis.
▼

Listed from outermost → deepest (remember: Come Let's Get Some Beer):

StratumNicknameKey Facts
Corneum"Horny layer"Outermost; dead keratinized cells; physical barrier to light, heat, microorganisms, and chemicals
Lucidum"Clear layer"Beneath corneum; only in thick skin (soles, palms); rows of dead flat keratinocytes; adds protection in high-friction areas
Granulosum"Granular layer"2–3 layers of flattened cells; where keratinization begins; cells fill with keratin, nuclei & organelles degrade, shape becomes compact and brittle
Spinosum"Spiny layer"Several layers of prickly/spiny polyhedral cells; desmosomes prevalent; contains dendritic cells (immune cells) that find/capture pathogens
Basale"Germinativum"Deepest layer; ONLY cells capable of dividing by mitosis; contains melanocytes
💡 Mnemonic — Outermost to Deepest"Come Let's Get Sun Baby" — Corneum · Lucidum · Granulosum · Spinosum · Basale
3
What is the difference between melanocytes and melanin?
▼
TermDefinition
MelanocytesCells found in the stratum basale that produce pigment (melanin)
MelaninThe pigment itself — absorbs and diffuses the sun's radiation to prevent it from damaging DNA in skin cells
💡 Simple RuleMelanocytes = the CELL · Melanin = the PRODUCT (pigment)
🔥 Burns, Hair & Glands
4
What is the difference between the degrees of burns?
▼
DegreeLayers InvolvedKey Features
First DegreeEpidermis onlyBrief contact with very hot/cold objects; e.g. sunburn; redness, pain, no blisters
Second DegreeEpidermis + dermis (minor dermal involvement)Redness, pain, swelling, blisters
Third DegreeEpidermis + dermis completely destroyedFull-thickness burn; usually painless because sensory receptors in skin are destroyed
⚠️ Key FactThird-degree burns are painless because sensory receptors are destroyed along with the skin layers.
5
What is hair composed of? What parts are visible? Explain goosebumps.
▼
3 Parts of a Hair Strand
PartDescription
CuticleOutermost portion; made of scalelike cells
CortexPrincipal portion; elongated, flattened cells united to form fibers
MedullaMiddle or central part of the hair
👁️ Visible PartThe shaft is the visible part of the hair — the portion that extends above the surface of the skin. The root is embedded in the follicle below the skin.
Arrector Pili Muscle — Goosebumps

The arrector pili muscle is an involuntary muscle attached to the hair follicle. When we are scared or cold, this muscle contracts and pulls on the hair follicle, causing the hair to "stand on its end" — producing the goosebump appearance on the skin.

6
Explain cyanosis and how to determine it in a dark-skinned person.
▼

Cyanosis is a bluish discoloration of the skin caused by a lack of oxygen in the blood.

Skin ToneHow Cyanosis Appears
Light-skinned individualsBluish tinge, most visible in lips, fingertips, nail beds
Dark-skinned individualsResults in a grayish/white tinge rather than blue — assess mucous membranes, nail beds, and palms
7
What are sebaceous glands? What do they produce? What is significant about the nail bed?
▼
Sebaceous Glands

Sebaceous glands develop along the walls of hair follicles and produce sebum — an oily secretion that lubricates the skin and hair, preventing dryness and providing a mild antimicrobial effect.

Nail Bed

The bed of the nail is where our nails grow. It contains blood vessels that give nails their pinkish color. It is assessed for circulation (capillary refill) and signs of cyanosis or clubbing.

💧 Sweat Glands & Medical Terminology

What is sodium chloride's role in sweat? What are the types of sweat glands?
▼
🧂 Sodium Chloride in SweatSodium chloride is present in sweat — this is why sweat tastes salty. It is released as part of the body's thermoregulation process.
Gland TypeLocationFunction
EccrineMost common; found all over the bodyProduce sweat and help regulate body temperature
ApocrineArmpits, genitalia, around anusProduce thicker secretions containing protein & fat; NOT involved in regulating body temperature
💡 MemoryEccrine = Everywhere (most common) · Apocrine = Armpits & private areas

Medical Terminology — Integumentary System
▼
Term/RootMeaningNotes
Chondr/oCartilagee.g., chondritis = inflammation of cartilage
Contus/oBruise / type of injuryContusion = a bruising injury from blunt force without breaking the skin; described as ecchymosis
EcchymosisBruiseDiscoloration of skin due to bleeding beneath the skin (bruise); used to describe the appearance of a contusion
ErythemaRedness of the skinCaused by dilation of capillaries (not just inflammation); the increased blood flow causes the visible redness
EdemaTissue swellingAccumulation of excess fluid in tissue spaces
PruritusItchingAssociated with dermatitis (skin inflammation)
⚠️ Key DistinctionsContus/o = the injury (contusion) · Ecchymosis = what you SEE (the bruise/discoloration) · Erythema = redness from capillary dilation

Quick Review — Key Integumentary Facts
▼
  • The epidermis has 5 stratums; the basale is the only one that divides by mitosis
  • The shaft is the visible part of the hair; the root is below the skin
  • The arrector pili muscle contracts to cause goosebumps (piloerection)
  • Third-degree burns are painless because sensory receptors are destroyed
  • Melanocytes produce melanin in the stratum basale
  • Eccrine glands are most common and regulate temperature; apocrine do not
  • Sebaceous glands produce sebum from hair follicle walls
  • Cyanosis in dark-skinned clients appears grayish/white — assess mucous membranes
  • Erythema = redness from dilation of capillaries
  • Ecchymosis = bruising (visual finding of a contusion)
